The Theoretical Landscape of Elementary Literacy

Professor's Note: These five models are not competing rules; they are different maps of the same territory. They help us visualize the complex, invisible process occurring in a student's mind as they learn to read.

The Simple View of Reading (Gough & Tunmer)

Reading comprehension is not a sum of skills, but a product of two distinct capacities. The critical insight is the multiplication sign: if either skill is zero, the outcome is zero.

Ehri's Phases of Word Reading

This model visualizes the journey toward "sight word" status. It is a progression of how effectively a reader connects sounds to letters to glue puzzles into instantly recognized pictures.

1
Pre-Alphabetic
Reads "McDonald's" logo, but not the text.
2
Partial Alphabetic
Sees "Puppy", says "Pet" (uses first letter).
3
Full Alphabetic
Sounds out C-R-U-S-T slowly but accurately.
4
Consolidated
Instantly sees chunks like "ing", "tion", "est".
Diagram Check: Notice the progression is not about memorizing whole words visually. It is about becoming increasingly efficient at mapping speech sounds to letters until the process becomes instantaneous (automaticity).

Scarborough's Rope

This is perhaps the most famous visual. It shows that skilled reading is the intertwining of many sub-skills. Crucially, the two main strands behave differently over time.

Dr. Strom's Reading Brain ("Brain City")

Modern neuroscience tells us humans are not born wired to read. We must recycle existing brain areas designed for vision, sound, and meaning, and build new "superhighways" connecting them.

Learning to read requires building a new area specifically for letters (often called the Visual Word Form Area, or "Letterbox") and physically growing neural connections to link vision to sound and meaning.
